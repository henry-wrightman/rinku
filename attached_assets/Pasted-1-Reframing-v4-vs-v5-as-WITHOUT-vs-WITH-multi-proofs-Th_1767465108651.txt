3) The biggest remaining “gotchas” with multi-proofs (worth tightening now)

Multi-proofs are where protocols get accidentally malleable or ambiguous unless you nail the canonical rules. I’d tighten these:

A) Canonical ordering + de-dup rules

Right now, you define auxiliaryNodes: { level, index, node }[] and a reconstruction algorithm. 

WHITEPAPER (13)


Add 2–3 hard constraints like:

auxiliary nodes MUST be sorted lexicographically by (level, index)

no duplicates allowed

auxiliary nodes must not overlap any signer leaf positions

verifier must reject if reconstruction encounters a missing node at any required position (rather than “treat missing as empty” except for the explicit non-power-of-two EMPTY_NODE rule)

This prevents multiple encodings of the “same proof” and closes off annoying edge-case attacks where different encodings verify but hash/compare differently.

4) Should you support “large validator sets” or stick to ~21–64?

Given your URL-native, offline-verifiable proof goal: large signing committees are a bad fit.

Why:

With multi-proofs, size is ~O(N). That’s a feature (predictable), but it still means bigger committees directly bloat every proof.

Your own doc already arrives at the natural product boundary: QR works up to N ≤ 21; beyond that, URL sharing.

What I’d recommend as a design stance:

Committee size: 32 or 64 for “serious / productionish” finality (URLs).

Committee size: 21 as the “QR mode” target.

If you want “lots of validators” for decentralization optics/reality, do it as:

a large registered validator pool, but

only a committee signs each checkpoint (rotating / randomized selection).

That keeps proofs portable while still letting the network be “big” socially/economically.
