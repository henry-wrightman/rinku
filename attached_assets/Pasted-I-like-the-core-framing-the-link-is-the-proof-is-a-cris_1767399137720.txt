Biggest blocker: “No external queries required” is only true under strict assumptions

You state “No external queries required. The URL is the proof.” 

WHITEPAPER


That’s conditionally true if the URL literally contains all bytes needed: ancestry transactions, checkpoint data, and membership proofs. 

WHITEPAPER

But in practice, two issues show up immediately:

1) URL size limits

A realistic “ancestry chain + merkle proof + checkpoint signatures” bundle will often be way bigger than what URLs tolerate across browsers, proxies, QR codes, messaging apps, and servers. Your own perf section says ~150–200 KB per transaction. 

WHITEPAPER


That’s not “URL-friendly” (and not QR-friendly either) unless you shard/segment and reassemble.

Recommendation: treat the URL as a content address (short) and make the “proof bundle” a retrievable blob (or an optional multi-part payload for QR).

2) Data availability is still a dependency

Even if verification doesn’t require a node you trust, it requires the data to exist somewhere. If you truly embed everything in the URL, that’s “availability via the sender” — fine — but then you hit the URL size wall above.

So in practice you’ll likely need:

a content network (DHT/IPFS/relays/archivers), or

“proof-serving” peers,

plus anti-eclipse assumptions.

Sybil resistance: “ageWeight” is easy to game unless you constrain it

Your weight formula mixes stake and “account age / oldest account age.” 

WHITEPAPER


Two problems:

Grinding: attackers can create many accounts and just wait (or buy old accounts).

Rich-get-richer / early adopter lock-in: oldest accounts permanently dominate the age term.

If you keep an age component, consider changing it to something harder to “farm,” like:

age of continuously staked stake (not just first tx timestamp),

capped/log-scaled age (so it saturates),

or “participation score” with decay.

Practical engineering notes

URLs as a transport layer are fragile. Some platforms truncate long URLs; some decode/normalize; some will choke on 100KB paths. If “URL-native” is the brand, you probably still want a short canonical link and optionally attach the proof bundle as a payload.

Compression choice: DEFLATE is fine, but for modern ratios/speed you might consider zstd/brotli depending on your environment (browser vs node).

Crypto: ECDSA P-256 is okay 

WHITEPAPER

, but most crypto ecosystems default to secp256k1 or Ed25519. If interoperability matters, justify P-256 and specify signature canonicalization (ECDSA malleability edge cases).
